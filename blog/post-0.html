Let's talk a bit about the Fort programming language.

The goal

To build something useful.

Ok, that's a little vague.

Specifically to build a programming language designed to be small and fast and safe. Something you would want running aboard the next flight you take. Or making that app on your phone faster. Or onboard a probe to Alpha Centauri. Or behind your favorite videogame.

Small, fast, and safe isn't enough though. Any programming language should also be convenient. Writing code is hard enough, the language should do what it can to minimize what Jonathan Blow refers to as friction.

Where did it come from?

The concept behind Fort started many, many years ago. I've always had a passion for programming but *cough*20*cough* years ago I was fortunate enough to work with a group that was very involved with the development and use of the Haskell programming language (check it out! http://www.haskell.org).

I still love and use Haskell (I'm developing Fort in Haskell) but oddly enough, my second favorite language is C. There's nothing like the speed and simplicity of C.

So there you have it, C-like performance with Haskell-like expressibility. That just seems very useful to me.

Where is it at?

Up to this point all of my work on Fort has been in-house. Exploritory, proof-of-concept experiments, so I could learn what worked and what didn't. What I felt was user friendly and what wasn't. This has been extremely valuable to me but I don't know that having a user base of one can be described as generally useful. Not to mention that this project would greatly benefit from feedback to help it grow and improve.

Inspired by Casey Muratori of Handmade Hero (http://handmadehero.org) and Andrew Kelley of Zig (http://ziglang.org) I've come to realize that usefulness can also come from the process. If the design and development of Fort can be educational, inspire you to write and release software, or you just find it curious, interesting, or perhaps even fun, that would be fantastic.

At the end of the day, I just want Fort to be useful. I think that's a pretty good goal.
